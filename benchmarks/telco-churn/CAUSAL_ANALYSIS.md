# Causal Analysis — Telco Churn (supplementary, not part of the Sealed Bet pipeline)

**What this is and isn't:** the Sealed Bet prediction pipeline (`sealed_bet/`,
`/ds-seal` → `/ds-auto` → `/ds-open`, see `last-ds-mile-run/`) answers *"who is likely to
churn"* — a ranking problem. This analysis answers a different question a retention team
also needs before spending budget: *"if we get a customer onto a longer contract, does
that itself causally reduce their churn risk, or are long-contract customers just
already the loyal ones?"* This is not a numbered pipeline stage (00–10) — it's a
standalone supplementary analysis, generated by `benchmarks/telco-churn/causal_analysis.py`.

**Why this exists:** an earlier pass considered `difference-in-differences` (the
`igerber/diff-diff` library) for this and concluded it didn't fit — DiD needs a
treatment/control assignment *and* pre/post time periods, and this is a single
cross-sectional snapshot with neither. On reconsideration with two libraries built for
exactly cross-sectional observational data — [DoWhy](https://github.com/py-why/dowhy)
(ATE estimation + refutation) and [CausalML](https://github.com/uber/causalml) (CATE /
heterogeneous treatment effects) — a real causal question does fit this dataset, and
both libraries produced genuine, actionable findings below.

## The causal question

**Treatment:** `long_contract` = 1 if `Contract` is `One year` or `Two year`, 0 if
`Month-to-month`.
**Outcome:** `Churn`.
**Confounders (adjusted for):** `tenure`, `SeniorCitizen`, `Partner`, `Dependents`,
`InternetService` — plausible common causes of *both* contract choice and churn (a
stable, loyal customer profile), not consequences of the contract itself.
**Deliberately excluded as confounders:** `MonthlyCharges`/`TotalCharges` and the add-on
services (`OnlineSecurity`, `TechSupport`, etc.) — contract terms often bundle pricing
and add-ons, so these are plausible *mediators* (part of the causal pathway from
contract → price/service → churn) rather than pre-treatment confounders. Adjusting for
a mediator would block part of the real effect and bias the estimate toward zero —
exactly the class of mistake this project's own `target-leakage-detection` discipline
exists to catch in the prediction pipeline; the same discipline applies here.

See `last-ds-mile-run/figures/causal-dag.png` for the DAG this analysis assumes.

## Finding 1 — most of the naive association is confounding, not a causal effect

| | Churn rate difference |
|---|---|
| Naive (unadjusted) | **−36.0pp** |
| Causal ATE (DoWhy, `backdoor.linear_regression`, adjusted for the confounders above) | **−14.0pp** |

See `last-ds-mile-run/figures/causal-ate-vs-naive.png`. Roughly 60% of the raw
"long-contract customers churn way less" association is explained by *who* chooses long
contracts (longer-tenured, more stable-household customers who were already unlikely to
churn) — not by the contract length itself. The remaining ~14pp is a real, if smaller,
causal effect: getting a customer onto a longer contract plausibly does reduce their
churn risk by something in that range, on average.

**Refutation checks (all pass):**
- **Placebo treatment** (permute `long_contract` randomly, re-estimate): effect collapses
  to **+0.3pp** — confirms the original −14.0pp isn't an artifact of the estimation
  procedure finding a "significant" effect out of noise.
- **Random common cause** (add an irrelevant random confounder): effect barely moves,
  **−14.0pp** — the estimate is stable to adding noise, as it should be if the real
  confounders are already captured.
- **Data subset** (re-estimate on 80% of the data): effect barely moves, **−14.1pp** —
  not driven by a handful of outlier rows.

## Finding 2 — the effect is far from uniform (CausalML T-learner CATE)

The DoWhy ATE above is one pooled linear estimate. A T-learner (two separate gradient-
boosted models, one fit on treated rows and one on control rows) captures nonlinearity
and interactions the linear ATE can't, at the cost of being a different estimator over a
slightly larger covariate set (also includes `InternetService` as dummies) — its overall
mean CATE (**−23.3pp**) isn't directly comparable to the linear ATE number, but both
agree on direction and general magnitude, which is reassuring triangulation from two
different methods rather than a contradiction.

**By `InternetService`** (`last-ds-mile-run/figures/causal-cate-by-segment.png`):

| InternetService | Mean CATE |
|---|---|
| Fiber optic | **−33.6pp** |
| DSL | −16.0pp |
| No internet | −14.5pp |

Fiber optic customers — already flagged in `02-explore.md` as a surprisingly high-churn,
price-sensitive segment — get more than **double** the causal benefit from a long-term
contract than DSL or no-internet customers. A retention offer built around contract
upgrades would have its biggest real impact targeted at fiber customers specifically,
not spread evenly.

**By tenure bucket:**

| Tenure | Mean CATE |
|---|---|
| 0–12 months | **−39.7pp** |
| 12–24 months | −22.2pp |
| 24–48 months | −16.7pp |
| 48–72 months | −12.0pp |

New customers get the largest causal benefit — intuitive, since they're in the highest-
risk churn window and have the most room to improve; long-tenured customers are already
unlikely to churn regardless of contract, so there's less effect left to capture.

## Practical implication

If this retention team is considering a "sign a longer contract, get a discount"
campaign: the honest expected impact is closer to **14pp average, and up to ~34–40pp for
new fiber customers specifically** — not the naive 36pp the raw correlation would
suggest. Targeting the campaign at new/fiber customers rather than broadcasting it
uniformly is where the causal analysis adds value the prediction model alone doesn't:
the Sealed Bet model ranks *who* is likely to churn; this ranks *where an intervention
would help most*.

## Why house-prices didn't get a symmetric causal analysis

No natural treatment variable maps onto that benchmark's framed decision (`00-frame.md`:
a suggested list price shown to a seller *before* a sale — a pure prediction problem, not
an intervention one). A defensible causal question could be constructed (e.g. "does
adding a garage causally increase sale price," relevant to a renovation-ROI product) but
that's a different product than the one framed, and forcing it in would be answering a
question nobody asked rather than the one `00-frame.md` actually poses. If a future run
wants that, it starts with reframing `00-frame.md`, not with picking a library.

## Reproducing this

```bash
python -m benchmarks.telco-churn.causal_analysis
```

Requires `dowhy` and `causalml` (not in `requirements-dev.txt` — these are specific to
this supplementary analysis, not the core plugin; install separately if reproducing).
Reads `last-ds-mile-run/dev.csv` (the sealed dev split); writes three figures to
`last-ds-mile-run/figures/`.
